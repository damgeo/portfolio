Tips for recognising bad behaviour
  • Is the person not listening to the speaker? Do you see them looking away and missing opportunities to observe the person's body language and facial expressions?
  • Are they not using the person's name when they address them?
  • Are they interrupting people talking?
  • Are they faced away from the speaker?
  • Are they folding their arms across their body or slouching?
  • Are they valuing what the other person is saying - listening to their opinion and responding by agreeing/presenting a valid counter-argument?

Asperger's and Hidden Disabilities

According to the National Autistic Society, Asperger syndrome is a lifelong developmental disability that affects how people perceive the world and interact with others. People with Asperger syndrome see, hear and feel the world differently to other people, and they are of average or above average intelligence. It is a sub-type of Autism.

It may not be easy to recognise someone with Asperger syndrome, Autism or any hidden disability, but if you treat people with respect and consider their needs, then it will be easier to discover how they would like to be treated and what additional help they may need.

Queen's University is committed to a policy of equal opportunity to Higher Education and strives to ensure that students with a disability or long term condition have equitable access to all aspects of university life. If you require additional support for a disability, please visit the Disability Services section of the Queen's University website.

Prejudice and stereotyping

We all carry assumptions, stereotypes and prejudices to make sense of our complicated world. When we do this in relation to people, we tend to form fixed ideas about who they are and what they are like - we may label or stereotype them.

For example:

  • Irish people like to drink.
  • Men are stronger than women and never cry.
  • Jews are greedy.
  • Muslims are terrorists.

Unconscious bias

According to the Equality Challenge Unit 'unconscious bias happens by our brains making incredibly quick judgements and assessments of people and situations without us realising. Our biases are influenced by our background, cultural environment and personal experiences. We may not even be aware of these views and opinions, or be aware of their full impact and implications'.

Unconscious biases are often based on our stereotypical views of people.

In order to combat this we need to avoid making quick judgements and slow the process down to ensure our background and personal experiences are not influencing our choices.
